Reproducing a reported bug is often the slowest part of triage, and judging
whether a report's steps to reproduce are executable is a manual and inconsistent
task. Whether a large language model can do this reliably enough to replace a
human is untested: prior work uses language models to improve or classify bug
reports but rarely validates them as reproducibility assessors against human
labels. We evaluate GPT-4o mini on 139 real Mojira (Minecraft) bug reports, in
original and quality-improved forms, labeling each report's steps to reproduce as
executable or non-executable and comparing against expert annotation with Cohen's
kappa on a protected holdout. On the holdout it reached $\kappa = 0.24$ on original
and $\kappa = 0.10$ on improved reports, far below our pre-registered $0.70$
threshold; an identical $0.68$ accuracy hid a near-constant executable verdict on
improved reports, and strong agreement seen during calibration did not transfer.
GPT-4o mini is therefore not yet dependable enough to replace human reproducibility
assessment in this setting, and agreement measured on calibration data overstates
real performance.
